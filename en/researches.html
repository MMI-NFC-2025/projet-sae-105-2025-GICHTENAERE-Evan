RESEARCHER

Who is she?

Born in 1912 in China, Chien-Shiung Wu grew up in a country where women’s access to science was rare. After outstanding studies, she moved to the United States, where she became a professor of physics at Columbia University. Her career was marked by unwavering determination in a field largely dominated by men.

What did she do?

A specialist in nuclear physics, Wu contributed to major breakthroughs through her high-precision experiments. Her research provided a better understanding of the behavior of matter and radioactivity, reinforcing her status as a leading reference in the scientific community.

A symbol of the Matilda Effect

View Glossary page (Matilda Effect)

Despite her central role in decisive discoveries, Wu did not receive the same recognition as her male colleagues. The most striking example was the experiment that revolutionized modern physics, for which her collaborators were awarded the Nobel Prize… without her. This silence surrounding her name perfectly illustrates the Matilda Effect: the erasure of women behind their male peers.

Beyond honors

The story of Chien-Shiung Wu is not only that of a great physicist. It is also a lesson in resilience. Set aside during a discovery she had irrefutably demonstrated, she never stopped moving forward. Twenty years later, new major contributions to physics earned her prestigious distinctions such as the National Medal of Science and the Wolf Prize in Physics.

This journey reminds us of a profound truth: the world’s approval is not an end in itself. Wu never worked for glory, but for the passion of understanding the universe.

“TIME’S JUSTICE PAYS TRIBUTE TO THOSE WOMEN WHO WERE MEANT TO BE ERASED.”

Her life shows that external recognition may take time, but that time ultimately delivers justice to those who pursue their path with sincerity. That is the true victory: living for what one loves, without waiting for validation from others.
